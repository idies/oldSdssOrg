TR97040
SDSS Trip Report
Telescope Control System Measurements
July 23 to 27, 1997

Claudio Rivetta and Sten Hansen
Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory
Batavia, IL   USA    60510

The trip was ok, we collected data and learned some things. There are several things I would like to discuss. Let me first to describe the trip and then we can talk about future actions.

On Wednesday, Angie Prosapio and I hooked up the amplifier and tachometer on the altitude axis and I took a lot of data. At night, very late, we saw some numbers that we did not like. On Thursday morning we found a moth squeezed on the altitude drive. After we cleaned the drive, numbers looked more reasonable.

On Thursday morning, I took more data from the altitude axis. During the afternoon, Angie and I connected all the stuff to the rotator. We took all the data necessary and left the system ready for some closed-loop tests in position.

On Friday, with Charlie at APO, we moved the rotator and he read the encoder with the MEI. All the pieces worked OK. During the afternoon we set and tuned the PID loop. In general it works OK. We found that the program for tuning the parameters of PID is not enough; we have to do something better. The other problem is due to the coupling of the Harmonic Drive. The motor shaft has an oscillatory response. You can not see the oscillations on the encoder on the instrument rotator, but by using the tachometer included with the reduction it is possible to see an oscillatory response. I'd like to feedback that signal to the controller either through the MEI or the summing node at the input of the Glentek Box. To do this requires running an extra shielded cable from the motor drive to the MEI or Glentek rack. I talked a little with Angie and Paul Czarapata about this. I'll talk with Paul in more detail later. I wanted to play a little more with my parameters in the computer but I had some problems with my simulation. It is a new homework, but we know that for Phase 1A or 1B the rotator can be moved and parked at some position using the MEI controller.

On Saturday, we started working on Azimuth with Sten Hansen while Angie and Charlie worked on the instrument lift. (I think this is the first time the schedule on the telescope overlapped between two groups!!!) We spent almost all morning troubleshooting the noise induced on the tach. At some point we decided to take some measurements with noise and then filter them out. The azimuth has almost twice as much friction as we recorded in January, and now the friction is symmetric, which is a good news. We also found that if someone was walking on the wind screen or on the building floor, the vibration was coupled to the telescope (the velocity signal from the tach. was showing noise in low frequency). Sten double-checked the coupling between the wind-screen and the telescope and we finally found that Carl Lindenmeyer put four tabs made of stainless steel on the telescope fork. These tabs span the gap between the fork and the wind-screen. They allow the instrument cart to move easily between the wind-screen and the fork. One of them was touching the wind screen floor and coupling the vibrations between both parts. We bent the tab up with a screwdriver and now you can jump on the wind-screen and not cause any output on the tach. signal. We lost one morning chasing down this problem, but we now know how sensitive the telescope is. After fixing the vibration problem, we took some additional drive data and redid some of our earlier measurements. Finally, we packed up our equipment.

I have discussed the tab problem with Carl and he is considering using another material for the tabs, or using another scheme. It is not a big deal. He raised a good question about what will happen when people start putting more stuff on the telescope and a lot of wire and hoses will couple the telescope with the building. The only thing I can say is that the control loop will reject only the low frequency vibration (up to 1Hz if we are optimists). The rest will be coupled in the same way the tab coupled the vibrations. We should monitor this every month with the encoder or the tachometer to verify whether the coupling is increasing as more cables are installed. In this way, we can check for solutions or improvements to reduce the coupling. Carl told me that French wants to seal the gap between the telescope and the wind-screen with 'something'. He should know about this problem and if he installs a seal, it will be prudent for us measure the coupling after installation.
