The Sky Survey Replaces its Monitor Telescope:
Problems With the Telescope

The Sloan Digital Sky Survey started having troubles with the monitor telescope shortly after its delivery. For example:

  • The encoders weren't wired properly, which means that the computer control system couldn't keep track of the telescope's position.
  • The telescope truss was constructed from aluminum, which expands and contracts with temperature more than most other materials. This made the telescope focus difficult to control; sharp images were all but unattainable.
  • A weight imbalance as small as one pound on either side of the fork mount would cause systematic slippage of the drive mechanism. Because the CCD cooling dewar boils off about a pound of liquid nitrogen every two hours, slippage was inevitable.
  • The telescope drive disks were made of soft aluminum instead of steel. These large disks were being driven by hard steel wheels that can easily deform and damage the disks.
  • There wasn't enough gear reduction between the drive motor and the telescope. As a result, the motor's (completely normal) small velocity variations caused unacceptably large errors in telescope tracking.

In short, the telescope manufacturer did not deliver a functioning instrument. John Briggs from the Yerkes Observatory and Tim McKay from the University of Michigan were called in to attempt to fix the telescope. Their years-long efforts seemed largely futile, however. The telescope wasn't reliable for more than a few weeks at a time, and its image quality was never as good as promised.

Despite all its flaws, the monitor telescope could actually produce data good enough for the Sky Survey's purposes. But the instrument required full-time maintenance, and no one was willing to bet that the telescope would remain fixable throughout the projected five-year span of the survey. (By contrast, most research telescopes last for 50 years or more.)
